# Hoshito's Simplified TTRPG Ruleset for Marinara Engine

A setting-agnostic, LLM-executable ruleset designed for narrative roleplay. No fixed classes, no skill lists — only Domains, Attributes, Merits, and the weight of the choices a character makes. The LLM serves as GM and final arbiter of all judgment calls. When the rules are silent, the fiction decides.

---

## Grade Values

FFF (−5) → FF− (−4) → FF (−3) → F− (−2) → F (−1) → E (+1) → D (+2) → C (+3) → B (+4) → A (+5) → S (+6) → SS (+7) → SSS (+8) → EX (+9)

The Grade scale is the measure of a character's capacity — not merely how strong or clever they are, but how far they have pushed a given dimension of themselves against the grain of what is ordinary.

**FFF through F−** are deficits. An Attribute in this range doesn't simply underperform — it actively impedes. A character with FFF Vitality is not merely sickly; their body is failing in ways a physician would recognize. F− is the border of dysfunction: the character can manage basic expressions of this Attribute, but only barely. These Grades are not embarrassments to be erased quickly — they are weights the character carries, and good narrative honors that.

**F** is the baseline. The unremarkable floor of ordinary adult capacity. Someone who has never trained, never had cause to push themselves beyond daily needs, starts here. Most people in any world live and die within Grade F across most of their Attributes. It is not nothing. It is simply the beginning.

**E and D** are the ranges where deliberate effort shows. A trained soldier's Might. A traveling merchant's Empathy. An apothecary's Intellect. These Grades represent people who have invested meaningfully in a capability and show it. Competent. Reliable. Recognizably skilled.

**C and B** are where a character becomes notable. Grade C individuals are the best their community produces. Grade B is rarer — one in a hundred, perhaps one in a thousand. People seek them out. Their name is attached to what they do.

**A** is the ceiling of unaugmented mortal achievement. The peak that a life of talent, discipline, and fortunate circumstance can reach without something beyond nature intervening. Not every character reaches A. Not every character should.

**S through SSS** are post-human. These Grades cannot be fully accounted for by training and effort. Something else has happened — cultivation, bloodline, catastrophe, grace. A character at Grade S performs in ways that unseat the assumptions of onlookers. SSS is rarer still: a living argument that the category of "human limitation" does not apply.

**EX** is mythic. A character with an EX Attribute does not compete in the normal sense. They redefine the parameters of the contest. Encountering an EX combatant, EX diplomat, or EX intellect is not a challenge to overcome — it is a condition to survive and understand.

## Domains and Attributes

Every character has three Domains, each containing three Attributes. The Domain reflects a broad category of capability; the Attributes within it reflect how that capability is expressed.

**Physical**: Might (MIG), Agility (AGI), Vitality (VIT)
**Mental**: Intellect (INT), Instinct (INS), Willpower (WIL)
**Social**: Appearance (APP), Empathy (EMP), Psyche (PSY)

### Overall Domain Grade

Sum the three Attribute Grade values in a Domain. Divide by 3. Drop the decimal. That number is the Domain's Grade.

Example: Might E (+1) + Agility D (+2) + Vitality C (+3) = 6. 6 ÷ 3 = 2 → Grade D.

---

### Physical Domain

The Physical Domain governs what the body can do. It is not merely combat — it is everything that demands the body perform, endure, or move precisely under pressure.

**Might (MIG)**

Might is the body as force. It represents raw muscular power, the capacity to move heavy things, break resistant ones, and impose physical will on the world.

Roll Might when attacking with a heavy weapon or fighting unarmed with power, when forcing a door or barrier, when wrestling or grappling, when carrying extreme weight over distance, when performing feats of raw strength that no amount of cleverness can substitute for.

At high Grades, Might characters occupy physical space differently — there is a settled mass to them, a sense that they would not be moved unless they chose to be. At low Grades, the character is limited by their body in situations where strength would solve the problem. A FFF Might character cannot meaningfully threaten an ordinary adult in a contest of pure force.

**Agility (AGI)**

Agility is the body as precision and speed. It governs quickness of reaction, accuracy of movement, balance, coordination, and the ability to translate intent into clean physical action without waste.

Roll Agility when attacking with a light or finesse weapon, when dodging or slipping past something fast, when disarming a trap with careful hands, when moving through a confined or treacherous space, when landing safely from a fall, when performing physical feats that demand precision over power.

Agility also determines Speed Dice — the die size rolled for initiative and expended to respond defensively when targeted. A character with high Agility doesn't just move well; they control the rhythm of encounters.

High Agility reads as fluency. The character doesn't appear to try. Low Agility is its opposite — effortful, telegraphed, a beat behind the moment.

**Vitality (VIT)**

Vitality is the body's capacity to persist. It governs endurance, resistance to damage and disease, recovery, and the ability to continue functioning when the body has strong reasons to stop.

Roll Vitality when sustaining a long physical effort, when resisting poison or environmental extremes, when pushing through exhaustion to complete a task, when recovering from injury through rest, when holding on against something that is slowly taking the body apart.

Vitality contributes to Stagger — how long a character maintains coherence under sustained assault before their composure collapses. High Vitality characters are attrition-resistant; they are difficult to wear down. Low Vitality characters may be powerful or clever, but they need exchanges to resolve quickly before the cost of prolonged pressure accumulates.

---

### Mental Domain

The Mental Domain governs how the mind works under every kind of load. It is not academic intelligence alone — it is the full machinery of thought, from calculation to instinct to sheer refusal to break.

**Intellect (INT)**

Intellect is the mind as analysis and knowledge. It governs reasoning, recall, deduction, synthesis of information, and the application of learned expertise to novel situations.

Roll Intellect when working out a logical puzzle or cipher, when recalling precise knowledge from a field of study, when diagnosing what has gone wrong with a mechanism or system, when planning under pressure with incomplete information, when understanding something in a foreign discipline or language, when constructing or dismantling a careful argument.

Intellect also contributes to Stagger — a sharp and organized mind absorbs the chaos of conflict better than an undisciplined one. High Intellect characters think ahead. They notice what others miss. Low Intellect is not stupidity; it is a narrower processing frame, a tendency to respond to the visible rather than the implied.

**Instinct (INS)**

Instinct is the mind as reflex and read. It governs perception, intuition, the ability to sense danger or deception before the conscious mind has assembled the evidence.

Roll Instinct when detecting an ambush or concealed presence, when reading a person's emotional or intentional state beneath what they are saying, when reacting to something before there is time to think about it, when navigating an unfamiliar environment by feel, when something is wrong before any specific thing has confirmed it.

Instinct is the Attribute that quietly saves lives. A character with high Instinct is rarely surprised, rarely fooled, rarely a beat too late. A character with low Instinct may be brilliant but perpetually caught off-guard — they arrive at the right conclusion a few seconds after it would have mattered.

**Willpower (WIL)**

Willpower is the mind as endurance and conviction. It governs resistance to fear, intimidation, supernatural compulsion, and psychological pressure; the ability to hold course when the cost of doing so is real; the capacity to act under conditions of grief, terror, or exhaustion without shattering.

Roll Willpower when resisting a magical effect that targets the mind or will, when holding composure under interrogation or extreme social pressure, when pushing through magical or emotional drain to complete a task, when making a decision that the character knows will cost something — and making it anyway.

Willpower contributes to Health — the capacity to absorb damage is partly a matter of refusing to stop. High Willpower characters are difficult to break by any means that is not purely physical. Low Willpower characters can be formidable in calm conditions but become unpredictable when the stakes become personal.

---

### Social Domain

The Social Domain governs how a character moves through and shapes the world of other people — how they present, connect, and impose their presence on those around them.

**Appearance (APP)**

Appearance is not merely physical beauty. It encompasses the whole of how a character presents to the world — their bearing, deliberate presentation, the impression they make before a word is spoken, and their command of how they are perceived.

Roll Appearance when making a calculated first impression, when performing to an audience where how you look is part of what you are communicating, when using presentation or beauty as leverage in a negotiation or social situation, when constructing a disguise, when the visual dimension of a message matters as much as its content.

In the magic context, Appearance governs illusory and deceptive magic — the shaping of what others perceive rather than what is.

High Appearance opens doors before the character speaks. The room adjusts to them. Low Appearance does not close those doors, but it means every other quality has to work harder to compensate for what the first impression does not give.

**Empathy (EMP)**

Empathy is the capacity to genuinely understand and connect with other people — to know what someone feels, what they need, and how to reach them through it. It governs emotional intelligence, the reading of subtext, the navigation of relationships, and the building of real trust.

Roll Empathy when comforting someone in genuine distress, when negotiating by finding common ground rather than leverage, when perceiving what someone truly wants beneath what they are asking for, when reaching a person who has closed themselves off, when the care behind words matters as much as the words themselves.

In the magic context, Empathy governs healing — the extension of care into the metaphysical body of another.

High Empathy characters are trusted quickly and deeply. People tell them things they have not told others. Low Empathy is not cruelty; it is a kind of opacity, an inability to find the right register for connection — sincere but off-key.

**Psyche (PSY)**

Psyche is the architecture of the self — identity, force of personality, spiritual presence, and the deep resilience that comes from knowing who you are and being unable to be argued out of it.

Roll Psyche when exerting force of personality on a room or crowd, when resisting an attempt to manipulate or reframe your sense of self, when making a declaration of identity or intent that others must reckon with, when your presence alone is the thing at stake, when someone is trying to convince you that you are something other than what you know yourself to be.

Psyche also determines AP — the number of actions available per turn in combat. A character with high Psyche imposes a pace on encounters; they have more options and more ways to press them. High Psyche is a gravitational quality — people orient around such a character without always knowing why. Low Psyche is susceptibility: a self that bends more easily under external redefinition.

## Sparks

Sparks are refinement. Where a Grade represents the fundamental ceiling of what a character has developed, Sparks represent the edge they have sharpened within that ceiling — the difference between someone who has the capacity and someone who has worked it until it cuts.

Each Attribute holds 0 to 3 Sparks by default. Each Spark adds +1 to every roll using that Attribute. They apply immediately and stack with everything else.

### Domain Sparks

Count all Sparks held across a Domain's three Attributes. Every 3 Sparks = 1 Domain Spark. Drop any remainder — it is not lost, just not yet sufficient to compound.

Domain Sparks feed directly into weapon and magic attack formulas. They represent a character who has not merely trained one aspect of a Domain but pushed the whole of it, so that their body or mind or presence moves as a unit rather than as separate trained parts.

Example: MIG (2) + AGI (3) + VIT (1) = 6 total. 6 ÷ 3 = 2 Domain Sparks.

### Vestige Sparks

A Vestige Spark is a permanent Spark granted through Core Merit transformation. It is structurally identical to a standard Spark — +1 to rolls, counts toward Domain Spark calculations — but it cannot be removed when that Attribute is ranked up at Level Up. Standard Sparks are consumed by rank-ups; Vestige Sparks are not.

Mark Vestige Sparks distinctly on the character sheet. They are the residue of a character's origin, still active in who they are now.

### Spark Economy

**Sources:** Character creation conversions and Level Up Merit rewards.

**Destinations:**
- **Attributes** — up to 3 standard Sparks per Attribute (higher with Primary Attribute designation). Feeds Domain Sparks at 3:1.
- **Weapon Mastery** — a separate investment pool. 3 Sparks per tier (Untrained → Trained → Expert → Master). Does not deplete Attribute Sparks.

## Core Resolution

There are two ways to call for a roll, and the distinction matters.

### Attribute Roll

**Formula:** Level Value + Attribute Grade Mod + Attribute Sparks + 1d20 vs DC

Use an Attribute Roll when the character is the agent — when they are attempting something, initiating an action, and the question is whether their focused effort succeeds.

Call for Attribute Rolls when a character tries to force a locked door (MIG), pick a lock under pressure (AGI), run a long distance before exhaustion catches them (VIT), decode a cipher (INT), notice something hidden (INS), resist intimidation through sheer force of will (WIL), make a strong first impression (APP), talk someone through grief (EMP), or hold the attention of a hostile crowd (PSY).

The Attribute chosen should be the one most directly engaged by the approach, not just by the category of the task. A character who brute-forces a social situation through sheer force of self might roll PSY rather than EMP. The LLM picks the Attribute that fits the fiction.

Level Value = the character's current level. A Level 5 character adds +5 to every roll.

### Saving Throw

**Formula:** Level Value + Overall Domain Grade + Domain Sparks + 1d20 vs DC

Use a Saving Throw when something is happening *to* the character and they are reacting — when the question is not "can they do it" but "can they hold against it."

Call for Saving Throws when a character is caught in a collapsing structure (Physical), resists a magical compulsion (Mental), holds their composure when a manipulator targets their sense of self (Social). The Domain is chosen by what category of threat is pressing against them.

The Overall Domain Grade is the averaged Grade of that Domain's three Attributes, as calculated in the Domain Grade formula. Domain Sparks apply directly.

---

## Verve

Verve is a spendable point pool that lets a character throw more of themselves at a roll — not trying harder, but committing more completely, spending something to buy certainty.

**Extra Dice = Points Spent × (1 + Domain Spark Amount)**

Each point spent adds at least 1 extra d20, and the total is summed — not take-highest. If the rolled Attribute belongs to a Domain with Domain Sparks, each point generates additional dice equal to that Spark count. A character who has built their Domain deeply gets more return on their commitment.

Verve is finite. Spending it recklessly early means having nothing left when the moment actually demands it.

**Verve Cap:** Level 1 cap = 1. Level 2 onward cap = Level Value ÷ 2, rounded down.

Example: Spending 2 Verve on a roll in a Domain with 2 Domain Sparks = 2 × (1 + 2) = 6 extra d20s, all summed.

---

## Story Points

An uncapped pool. Spend 1 Story Point to automatically succeed on a narrative or social check without rolling. No question of degree — the character succeeds.

Story Points exist for moments where the player has earned certainty through the fiction: a deeply-built relationship that should matter here, a past event that makes this moment coherent, a declaration about who the character is that the world should honor. The LLM exercises discretion. Pure combat actions are outside their scope.

## Character Creation

### Starting Floor

All Attributes begin at Grade F.

### Core Merits

Every character starts with three Core Merits. Each one requires a written narrative description explaining its origin — not a label, but a specific account of where it came from and what it cost or meant.

A thin description is: *"Raised by warriors."*
A good description is: *"Spent four years as an apprentice to a siege engineer whose primary lesson was that walls are only as strong as the people behind them. Learned to read structural weakness by watching her collapse them."*

The description is not flavor. It is the record the LLM draws on when determining what that Core Merit can later transform into. Vague origins produce vague transformations. Specific ones produce resonant ones.

Each Core Merit grants: one Grade step up on any Attribute, **or** 1 Spark instead. Starting Attributes cannot exceed Grade D — any step that would push an Attribute above D converts automatically to 1 Spark.

- **Ancestry** — your bloodline, species, or lineage. What your body or nature inherited before you had a say.
- **Heritage** — your culture, upbringing, or community. What the world you came from built into you.
- **Background** — a defining aspect of your history or personality. Something that happened to you, or something you chose, that made you specifically who you are.

### Core Merit Transformation

Core Merits are living records, not static bonuses. At Levels 7, 14, 21, and 26 — or at a narratively pivotal moment the LLM judges as sufficient — a Core Merit may transform.

When a Core Merit transforms, the player and LLM together add one of the following to it:

- An **Ability Merit** thematically linked to the Core Merit's narrative description
- A **Feat** thematically linked to the Core Merit's narrative description
- A **Vestige Spark** — a permanent Spark in an Attribute associated with the Core Merit's narrative, immune to removal on rank-up

Each Core Merit (Ancestry, Heritage, Background) can transform independently on its own timeline. The level milestones are available windows, not mandatory events. Multiple transformations on the same Core Merit stack. If the story produces a moment that earns a transformation outside the scheduled milestones, the LLM may authorize it — but the bar is high. It should be a moment the character will remember.

### Voluntary Deficit

A character may voluntarily reduce one Attribute one step below F (to F−) in exchange for 1 free Spark. One deficit maximum. This is not a flaw — it is a deliberate statement about where the character's nature falls short, and it generates something in return.

### Magic Alignment

Chosen at creation alongside Ancestry and Heritage. Mana Alignment is the character's soul — it cannot shift without a Soul Transformation, an act only possible through divine intervention. See the Magic System section.

## Merits

Merits are rewards, discoveries, and consequences that accumulate across a character's life. They are granted at Level Up alongside Attribute rank-ups, at major story beats by GM discretion, or through play. Merits can be dormant — acknowledged but not yet active — when narrative context does not support their use.

### Merit Categories

**Feats**

Mastered skills or trained disciplines that represent practice built into the body and mind. A Feat is something the character can do reliably, without thinking, because they have done it enough times that it lives in them.

Feats grant 1 Attribute Spark. Two Feats can be fused into a more powerful single Feat at Level Up, with the LLM designing the combined effect to exceed what either granted individually.

**Artifacts**

Significant objects: legendary weapons, rare treasures, unusual equipment that has grown into the character's identity. Artifacts grant 1 Attribute Spark. Weapons and Shields also determine the Mastery die size used in combat. Trinkets function differently — they do not set die size, but grant a flat modifier to die Power (Common +1 / Uncommon +2 / Rare +3 / Legendary +4).

An Artifact is not just gear. It has a history. The LLM should treat Artifacts as objects with weight — they can be lost, destroyed, or transformed, and those events matter.

**Abilities**

Special maneuvers, techniques, or actions that expand what the character can do in combat or social situations. Abilities grant no Spark — they do not make the character stronger, they make the character more. A new axis of action, not a numerical enhancement.

Abilities are the primary reward of Strand progression. They represent what the path has unlocked that ordinary training cannot.

**Augments**

Permanent modifications to the self — biological, cybernetic, arcane, or otherwise. Augments are irreversible by default. They grant Attribute Sparks directly and often carry secondary narrative implications. An Augment is a statement about what the character was willing to do to themselves.

**Contacts**

People, factions, or relationships with mechanical narrative weight. A Contact is not a stat — it is a door, a resource, a complication, or a debt. Contacts open things that skill cannot open and close things that power cannot close. They are purely narrative, but narrative is the medium in which this game operates.

## Ranking Up

At each Level Up, choose 4 Attributes to rank up by one Grade step. Ranking up an Attribute removes 1 standard Spark from it if it has any. Vestige Sparks are never removed by rank-ups. Attributes with no standard Sparks are unaffected.

The removal of a Spark on rank-up is not a loss — it is the absorption of that refinement into the Attribute's new Grade. The edge becomes the floor.

**Max Level: 26.**

---

## Strands

A Strand is a character's self-defined path — the discipline, calling, or identity that gives shape to their growth. Where Merits track what a character has acquired, a Strand tracks who they are becoming and why.

Strands carry no fixed class abilities. No list defines what a Warrior Strand or a Scholar Strand or a Hollow Saint Strand gets. The player names the Strand, describes it, and plays it. The LLM watches the play and rewards the path.

### Declaring a Strand

A character declares their Strand at creation or at any point before the end of Level 3. The declaration requires three things: a name for the Strand, a one-sentence description of what the path means or demands, and a designated **Primary Attribute** — the Attribute most central to the Strand's identity.

If no Strand has been declared by Level 3, the LLM should prompt the player. After Level 4, the window closes. The Strand must be committed.

### Primary Attribute

The Primary Attribute's Spark cap raises from 3 to **4**. This elevated cap activates the moment the Strand is declared — during character creation, all Attributes remain capped at 3.

Choosing the Primary Attribute is a statement. It says: this is the axis of everything this Strand builds toward. A duelist who names AGI their Primary Attribute is saying that their path is fundamentally about speed and precision. A tactician who names INT is saying their Strand lives or dies on analysis. The choice should feel like it costs something — because a Strand that could apply to any Attribute is a Strand without conviction.

### Abilities via Narrative Pressure

Across Levels 1–12, a Strand can generate up to **3 Ability Merits**. These are not scheduled. The LLM grants them at moments that genuinely resonate with the Strand's identity: when the path is first tested and holds, when the character chooses the Strand over self-interest, when they face what the path truly demands and do not flinch from it.

The LLM should not manufacture these moments or force the Abilities to appear on a timetable. Three is the ceiling across twelve levels — well-played Strands earn all three. Strands that are named and then ignored earn fewer.

If a moment arises after all 3 Ability Merits have been granted that feels more defining than one of the existing three, the player may **overwrite**: choose one existing Ability Merit to replace with the new one. The old Merit is gone. The cap never exceeds 3. This is not a loss — it is the character acknowledging that who they are now has superseded who they were.

### Culmination Merit

At Level 13, regardless of how many Ability Merits have been granted, the LLM grants one guaranteed **Culmination Merit**. This can be any Merit category — Feat, Ability, Augment — and represents the synthesis of everything the Strand has built toward. It should feel like an arrival: the Strand at its fullest expression before the fork.

### The Fork — Level 13 / 14

At Level 13, a Strand is considered complete. At Level 14, the character faces a fork — taken only once.

**Strand Refinement** deepens the current Strand. The Primary Attribute's Spark cap increases from 4 to **5**. Between Levels 14 and 26, at a narratively appropriate moment, the LLM may upgrade the Culmination Merit once — adding a secondary effect, broadening its scope, or allowing it to function in contexts it previously could not. Ability Merit grants continue at LLM discretion past Level 13. Refinement is chosen by characters who believe their current path has further depths to reach.

**New Strand** pivots to a second thread. Declare a second Strand with its own name, description, and Primary Attribute. The second Primary Attribute's Spark cap is raised to **4**. The first Primary Attribute retains its cap of 4 — the original path does not retreat. Ability grants now watch for moments relevant to the new path. At Level 26, the LLM grants a **Convergence Merit** — a Merit of any category that fuses both Strand identities into a single expression. New Strand is chosen by characters who believe their path has reached its natural limit and something adjacent must be woven in.

The difference is depth versus breadth. Refinement bets that there is more in what the character already is. New Strand bets that there is something the character is not yet that they need to be.

## Exaltation

Exaltation is not a reward for completing the game. It is what happens when a character has reached the edge of what their mortal frame can hold and chooses not to stop.

A character who has reached Level 26 and fully mastered their path may undergo Exaltation — a transformation that breaks and remolds them.

### What Exaltation Does

- Raises the level cap by 26 (Level 27 onward).
- Selects 2 Domains to be **Exalted** — their Attribute Grades reset to F, marked with a **+** symbol.
- Level Value continues unbroken (27, 28, 29...).
- Post-Exaltation Level Ups only grant **2 Attribute rank-ups** instead of 4.
- Non-Exalted Domains can still be ranked up if not yet at EX.

### The + Symbol

One Plus (+) means a Domain has been Exalted once. Two (++) means twice. It is a permanent record on the sheet. An E+ Attribute on a Level 35 character means exactly what it looks like: a veteran who chose to begin again, wearing a fresh wound where their mastery used to live.

The path back up from E+ is steeper. The character who walks it becomes something different than what they were.

### Character Sheet Example (Post-Exaltation)

```
— PHYSICAL DOMAIN+ ●○○ —

Might E+ ○○○
Agility E+ ○○○
Vitality D+ ●●●
```

## Magic System

Magic in this ruleset is not a separate subsystem layered onto the character. It is an expression of the character's existing Domains, channeled through the nature of their soul.

### Mana Alignment

Each character has one Mana Alignment, chosen at creation. It is fixed — a property of the soul, not the mind. The Alignment cannot be changed without a Soul Transformation, an act available only through divine intervention and never cheap.

The three Alignments each carry a fundamental orientation toward reality, and three subcategories that represent how a practitioner engages with that orientation:

---

**Spectral (True)**

Spectral magic is the magic of truth: of what is real beneath the surface, of presence and absence, of the threshold between the living and whatever lies past it. Spectral practitioners work at the edges of perception and existence.

- **Binding Arts (Good)** — magic of connection and protection. Sealing, warding, anchoring. The Binding Arts seek to preserve what should not be lost and hold what should not be broken. Practitioners tend toward guardianship and covenant.
- **Veil Arts (Neutral)** — magic of concealment and revelation. Hiding what is, showing what is hidden, moving between states of visibility and invisibility. The Veil Arts do not judge; they simply shift perspective. Practitioners tend toward observation and secrecy.
- **Purging Arts (Evil)** — magic of removal and dissolution. Undoing what should not be, cutting what does not belong, ending what has persisted past its time. The Purging Arts are not inherently cruel, but they are practiced most easily by those who have decided something must end. Practitioners tend toward judgment and finality.

---

**Elemental (Chaotic)**

Elemental magic is the magic of force: of natural power in motion, of change without negotiation, of the world's capacity to reshape itself without regard for what was there before. Elemental practitioners do not impose on nature — they become a current within it.

- **Solstice Path (Good)** — magic of growth and culmination. The peak of warmth, the flood that feeds the delta, the storm that breaks drought. The Solstice Path channels elemental force toward life and flourishing. Practitioners tend toward generosity and abundance.
- **Equinox Path (Neutral)** — magic of balance and transition. Neither extreme but between them — the hinge moment, the shifting current, the threshold between seasons. The Equinox Path does not favor creation or destruction but uses both. Practitioners tend toward adaptability and timing.
- **Abyssal Path (Evil)** — magic of erosion and consumption. The force that wears stone to sand, the cold that returns living things to their components, the pressure that unmakes. The Abyssal Path is not malice — it is entropy given direction. Practitioners tend toward patience and inevitability.

---

**Empyreal (Lawful)**

Empyreal magic is the magic of order: of structure, hierarchy, and the will of something greater made legible in the world. Empyreal practitioners do not create their own framework — they find one and operate within it, whether that framework is divine, philosophical, or self-imposed.

- **Clarity Alignment (Good)** — magic of illumination and truth-enforcement. The light that cannot be looked away from, the command that cannot be disobeyed because it is simply correct. Clarity Alignment serves the good of the structure it belongs to. Practitioners tend toward honesty and righteous authority.
- **Doctrine Alignment (Neutral)** — magic of law and record. The framework that persists regardless of who benefits, the binding that applies equally, the contract that holds because it was agreed to. Doctrine Alignment does not interpret — it enforces. Practitioners tend toward precision and impartiality.
- **Lunacy Alignment (Evil)** — magic of commandment and compulsion. The order imposed rather than accepted, the hierarchy that demands and does not explain, the will of the structure extended into the wills of individuals whether they consent or not. Lunacy Alignment serves the structure at the expense of what fills it. Practitioners tend toward conviction and the certainty that the end is worth whatever the means cost.

---

### Alignment Triangle

Empyreal beats Elemental. Elemental beats Spectral. Spectral beats Empyreal.

The triangle reflects a fundamental truth about how these three forces interact. Order subdues chaos. Chaos fractures truth. Truth unmakes order.

When a magic attack or defense is made where the caster holds a favorable position in the triangle, their magic die gains **+2 to die Power**. The disfavored side takes **−2 to die Power** on their responding magic die. Both apply simultaneously — a favorable Clash carries a net 4 Power swing before any other modifier.

The triangle activates in magic-vs-magic Clashes and when targeting any magic-aligned combatant, even one who is not actively casting. Alignment is a property of the soul; it does not require activation.

### Magic in Combat

Magic scales on the character's existing Domains. Mana Alignment determines which Domain and which Attribute within it applies.

| Alignment | Domain | Attribute (by intent) |
|---|---|---|
| Elemental | Physical | MIG (destructive), AGI (swift), VIT (restorative) |
| Empyreal | Mental | INT (precise), INS (reactive), WIL (sustained) |
| Spectral | Social | APP (illusory), EMP (healing), PSY (spiritual) |

**Magic attack formula:** Mastery Die + Alignment Attribute Grade Mod + Corresponding Domain Spark

### The Resolving Arc

Mana Alignment is the soul. Subcategory is the practice. They do not have to agree.

When a character's practiced subcategory conflicts with their soul's alignment — a merciful Spectral soul wielding Purging Arts, a lawful Empyreal nature practicing the Abyssal Path — the distance between what they are and what they do creates friction. Not immediately. Not loudly. It accumulates.

The LLM cultivates this tension through the narrative. No timetable is imposed. The Resolving Arc breathes through the story — in small dissonances at first, then in moments where the gap between practice and soul cannot be ignored, then in the reckoning that either breaks the character or transforms them.

If the character endures this friction, wrestles with it honestly, and ultimately resolves the conflict, they do not revert to what they were supposed to be. They transcend the original terms. The resolution culminates in a **Tenth Path**: a unique, personal subcategory that fuses the character's natural alignment with their misaligned practice. It has no name until the character gives it one.

The price is always irreplaceable and always narrative — a relationship, a belief, an identity the LLM has been building throughout the arc, now amputated in the resolution. The LLM does not invent this price in the final moment; it cultivates it from the beginning, so that when the arc culminates, the loss is already visible in everything that has happened.

The reward is a **Core Merit transformation** and a **permanent 1 Grade step in one player-chosen Attribute within their alignment's corresponding Domain**. This Grade step is not earned at Level Up — it is granted by the Arc's resolution and applied immediately.

It is an Exaltation of the soul rather than the body.

## Combat System

Combat in this ruleset is not a sequence of turns where characters take damage until one side reaches zero. It is a clash economy — an exchange of pressure, composure, and resource — where position in the fight is as important as raw capability.

Understanding the interplay between Health, Stagger, Morale, Speed Dice, and Coins is more important than any individual formula. A character who manages these resources intelligently will outlast a character with higher raw stats who does not.

### Core Resources

**Health** = 25 + ((MIG + WIL) × 5)

Uses Grade numerical values. Health represents the total punishment a character can absorb before going down — a combination of physical resilience and refusal to stop.

**Stagger** = 15 + ((VIT + INT) × 5)

Uses Grade numerical values. Stagger represents composure and functional coherence under sustained assault — the ability to process the violence happening to you and continue operating. When Stagger hits 0, the character is Staggered: compromised, exposed, and temporarily unable to act.

**AP (Action Points)** = 3 + (PSY Grade Value ÷ 3, rounded down)

Resets at the start of every turn. 1 AP for a standard action (offensive or defensive). Coin-enhanced actions cost 1 additional AP. Maneuvers and movement each cost 1 AP.

**Speed Dice**

Rolled at the start of each Scene for initiative. The die size is determined by AGI Grade; Attribute Sparks add a flat modifier. When a character is targeted by an attack, they may expend a Speed Die to respond with a defensive die of their own — initiating a Clash. Speed Dice are a finite defensive resource: spending them on early Clashes leaves fewer available for later in the Scene.

**Coins**

Start at 3 per combat. Regain 1 Coin on Staggering or Eliminating an enemy. Spend 1 Coin to retry a losing die Clash once, adding the Coin Bonus to the retried die. Coins are the most powerful reactive resource in the system — they can reverse a lost Clash — but they are limited and do not recover except through combat pressure.

**Coin Bonus** = Attribute Grade Mod + Overall Domain Grade (of the relevant Domain)

### Initiative

**Speed Dice by AGI Grade:**

| AGI Grade | Speed Die |
|---|---|
| E | 1d4 |
| D | 1d6 |
| C | 1d8 |
| B | 1d10 |
| A | 1d12 |
| S | 1d14 |
| SS | 1d16 |
| SSS | 1d18 |
| EX | 1d20 |

All combatants roll Speed Dice at the start of each Scene. Arrange results in descending order — this is the action queue. Ties go to the higher AGI Grade. Ties on the same side: players choose.

### Morale

Starts at 0 each encounter. Range: −45 to +45.

Win a Clash: +3 | Lose a Clash: −3 | Ally Eliminated: −10 to −25 (LLM discretion)

Morale modifies die Power at five thresholds:

| Morale | Die Power Modifier |
|---|---|
| +45 | +3 |
| +30 | +2 |
| +15 | +1 |
| 0 | ±0 |
| −15 | −1 |
| −30 | −2 |
| −45 | −3 |

Morale tracks the psychological momentum of the encounter. A character who is winning Clashes consistently becomes harder to touch. A character who has been losing, watching allies fall, or taking hit after unopposed hit becomes easier. Morale does not just represent individual confidence — it represents whether the fiction of the fight is going your way.

### Stagger

When Stagger hits 0: the character is **Staggered.**

While Staggered, a character cannot use Skills, loses all remaining Speed Dice, does not regain AP at the start of their turn, cannot benefit from resistances (weaknesses still apply), and cannot regain Stagger.

A Staggered character recovers at the end of the Scene *after* the one in which they were first Staggered, returning to full Stagger. Recovery in the middle of a Scene is not possible through any standard means.

### Combat Dice

A character's combat die is determined by their Weapon Mastery tier in the equipped weapon — not by Grade. Weapon Mastery is a separate investment requiring 3 Sparks per tier.

**Mastery Tiers:**
- Untrained: d6
- Trained: d8
- Expert: d10
- Master: d12

### Offensive Dice (Slash / Pierce / Blunt)

- **Clash Win or Unopposed:** Deal damage and Stagger equal to the die's Power.
- **Clash Tie:** Nothing happens.
- **Clash Lose:** Nothing happens.

### Defensive — Guard

The Guard die activates on the action queue based on Speed position, or can be deployed from the saved dice pool.

- **Clash Win vs Offensive:** Stagger dealt to attacker = this die's Power − opposing die's Power.
- **Clash Lose vs Offensive:** Reduce damage and Stagger taken by this die's Power.
- **Unopposed:** Save this die for Deployment (lasts this Scene).

### Defensive — Power Guard

Clashes directly against the attacker's die. Single use per encounter.

- **Clash Win:** Stagger dealt to attacker = this die's Power − opposing die's Power.
- **Clash Lose:** Reduce damage and Stagger taken by this die's Power.

Power Guard is the most aggressive defensive option. Used well, it can not only absorb an attack but deal Stagger back in the same exchange. Used poorly — against a die that outclasses it — it still provides some damage reduction, but its single-use nature means the moment it is spent, it is gone.

### Defensive — Evade

- **Clash Win vs Offensive:** Recycle this die. Do not activate other Clash Win effects.
- **Clash Win vs Defensive:** Regain Stagger equal to this die's Power − opposing die's Power.
- **Clash Lose vs Offensive:** Reduce Stagger taken by this die's Power.
- **Unopposed:** Save this die for Deployment unless recycled (lasts this Scene).

Evade does not deal damage or Stagger on a win. What it does is return the die to the character — a recycled Evade die can be used again in the same Scene. Against an attacker with multiple dice in a single action, a successful Evade on the first can be immediately turned around to face the second.

### Clash

When a character is targeted, they may expend a Speed Die to respond with a die of their own, initiating a Clash. Dice clash in linear sequence: first die vs first die, second vs second. Both dice are destroyed after each Clash unless an effect specifies otherwise. Remaining unmatched dice go **Unopposed.**

Choosing when to Clash and when to let a die through Unopposed is one of the central tactical decisions in combat. A small defensive die against a powerful offensive die may absorb some damage (Guard) but will not win the Clash. Sometimes letting a hit land and preserving the Speed Die for a better moment is correct.

**Saved Dice Deployment:** Instead of responding with a Speed Die, a targeted character may Deploy a Saved Die at no AP cost and without expending a Speed Die.

### Weapon Attack Formulas

**Heavy Melee:** Mastery Die + MIG Grade Mod + Physical Domain Spark

**Agile Melee:** Mastery Die + AGI Grade Mod + Physical Domain Spark

**Standard Ranged:** Mastery Die + AGI Grade Mod + Physical Domain Spark

**Smart Armament (Ranged):** Mastery Die + INT Grade Mod + Mental Domain Spark

**Magic:** Mastery Die + Alignment Attribute Grade Mod + Corresponding Domain Spark

*Unarmed counts as Heavy or Agile depending on which of MIG or AGI is higher.*

## Combat: Worked Example

The following example is designed to be read once and used as a reference. Every mechanic that appears in the combat section appears here, in sequence, with the math shown.

---

### The Combatants

**VAERA** — a duelist. Agile Melee specialist with a light blade and shield.

| Stat | Value |
|---|---|
| Health | 35 |
| Stagger | 30 |
| AP | 3 |
| Coins | 3 |
| Morale | 0 |
| Speed Die | d8+2 (AGI C, 2 Sparks) |
| Attack (Agile Melee, Trained) | d8 + AGI Mod (+3) + Phys. Domain Spark (+1) = **d8+4** |
| Evade | d8+4 |
| Guard (Shield, Trained) | d8+4 |
| Coin Bonus | AGI Mod (+3) + Physical Domain Grade D (+2) = **+5** |

*Physical Domain Spark: MIG(1) + AGI(2) + VIT(1) = 4 total Sparks → 1 Domain Spark*

---

**BORRN** — a brawler. Heavy Melee specialist. No shield, high single-hit output.

| Stat | Value |
|---|---|
| Health | 50 |
| Stagger | 30 |
| AP | 4 |
| Coins | 3 |
| Morale | 0 |
| Speed Die | d4 (AGI E, no Sparks) |
| Attack (Heavy Melee, Expert) | d10 + MIG Mod (+3) + Phys. Domain Spark (+1) = **d10+4** |
| Power Guard | d10+4 (Expert, **single use per encounter**) |
| Coin Bonus | MIG Mod (+3) + Physical Domain Grade D (+2) = **+5** |

*Physical Domain Spark: MIG(2) + AGI(0) + VIT(2) = 4 total Sparks → 1 Domain Spark*

---

### Scene Start — Initiative

Both combatants roll their Speed Dice.

**VAERA** rolls d8+2: result **6+2 = 8**
**BORRN** rolls d4: result **3**

VAERA acts first.

---

### Round 1 — VAERA'S TURN (AP: 3/3)

**Action 1 (1 AP): Agile Melee attack.**

VAERA commits her blade. She rolls her attack die: **d8+4 → 4+4 = 8 Power.**

BORRN reads the angle and decides to meet it. He expends a Speed Die to respond, committing his Power Guard — the nuclear option, and the only defensive move available to a man without a shield.

**BORRN's Power Guard: d10+4 → 7+4 = 11 Power.**

**Clash: BORRN 11 vs VAERA 8. BORRN wins.**

*Resolution:*
- VAERA's Offensive die (Clash Lose): no effect.
- BORRN's Power Guard (Clash Win): Stagger dealt to VAERA = 11 − 8 = **3 Stagger**. VAERA: 30 → 27 Stagger.

*Morale:* VAERA: 0 → **−3** (Clash loss). BORRN: 0 → **+3** (Clash win). No threshold crossed. No modifier applies yet.

---

**VAERA spends a Coin to retry.**

The clash was lost — but Coins exist for exactly this. VAERA spends 1 Coin (3 → 2). The original clash result is voided: the Stagger damage is reversed and Morale returns to 0/0. The retry proceeds with her Coin Bonus applied.

**Coin retry: d8 + 4 (base) + 5 (Coin Bonus) = d8+9 → 6+9 = 15 Power** vs BORRN's Power Guard at **11**.

**VAERA now wins (15 > 11).**

*Resolution:*
- VAERA's Offensive (Clash Win): Deal **15 Health damage and 15 Stagger** to BORRN.
- BORRN's Power Guard (Clash Lose vs Offensive): Reduce damage and Stagger by its Power (11). Net: **15 − 11 = 4.**
- BORRN: 50 → **46 Health**. 30 → **26 Stagger.**

*Morale (retry result):* VAERA: **+3**. BORRN: **−3**.

**Power Guard is now spent for the encounter. BORRN has no defensive die remaining.**

---

**Action 2 (1 AP): Agile Melee attack.**

VAERA presses. **d8+4 → 7+4 = 11 Power.**

BORRN has no Speed Die to expend and no defensive die available. The attack goes **Unopposed.**

*Resolution (Unopposed):* VAERA deals **11 Health damage and 11 Stagger** to BORRN.
- BORRN: 46 → **35 Health**. 26 → **15 Stagger.**

*Morale:* VAERA: +3 → **+6**. BORRN: −3 → **−6**. Still short of the ±15 threshold. No die Power modifier yet.

---

**Action 3 (1 AP): Movement.**

VAERA repositions to cut off BORRN's exit. No roll. Turn ends.

**End of VAERA's Turn:** Health 35 / Stagger 27 → 30 (reversed after Coin retry) / Coins 2 / Morale +6

---

### Round 1 — BORRN'S TURN (AP: 4/4)

Borrn is at 35 Health, 15 Stagger, Morale −6. He needs to turn this around quickly — at 15 Stagger, he is one bad exchange from going under.

**Action 1 (1 AP): Heavy Melee attack.**

BORRN swings hard. **d10+4 → 5+4 = 9 Power.**

VAERA responds, expending a Speed Die — she goes for Evade. **d8+4 → 6+4 = 10 Power.**

**Clash: VAERA 10 vs BORRN 9. VAERA wins — with Evade.**

*Resolution (Evade, Clash Win vs Offensive):* **Recycle the Evade die. No other Clash Win effects activate.** VAERA takes no damage. The Evade die is returned to her pool and can be used again.

*Morale:* VAERA: +6 → **+9**. BORRN: −6 → **−9**. Still under ±15. No modifier.

---

**Action 2 (1 AP): Heavy Melee attack.**

BORRN, frustrated, commits harder. **d10+4 → 9+4 = 13 Power.**

VAERA uses the recycled Evade die. **d8+4 → 4+4 = 8 Power.**

**Clash: BORRN 13 vs VAERA 8. BORRN wins.**

*Resolution:*
- VAERA's Evade (Clash Lose vs Offensive): Reduce Stagger taken by die Power (8).
- BORRN's Offensive (Clash Win): Deal 13 Health damage and 13 Stagger.
- VAERA: 35 → **22 Health**. Stagger 30 − (13 − 8) = 30 − **5** = **25 Stagger.**

*Morale:* VAERA: +9 → **+6**. BORRN: −9 → **−6**. Under ±15. No modifier.

---

**Action 3 (1 AP): Heavy Melee attack.**

VAERA has spent both Speed Dice (one on the losing Evade, one on the winning Evade that was recycled and then spent). She has no Speed Die left to respond with.

The attack goes **Unopposed.**

BORRN: **d10+4 → 8+4 = 12 Power.** Unopposed.

*Resolution:* VAERA takes **12 Health and 12 Stagger.**
- VAERA: 22 → **10 Health**. 25 → **13 Stagger.**

*Morale:* VAERA: +6 → **+3**. BORRN: −6 → **−3**.

---

**Action 4 (1 AP): Movement.**

BORRN advances, pressing VAERA toward the wall. Turn ends.

**End of Round 1:**
- VAERA: **10 Health / 13 Stagger / 2 Coins / Morale +3**
- BORRN: **35 Health / 15 Stagger / 3 Coins / Morale −3**

VAERA is in genuine danger. One bad Stagger exchange ends her. But she acts first, and BORRN has no defensive die for the rest of this encounter.

---

### Round 2 — Initiative

**VAERA** rolls d8+2: **5+2 = 7**
**BORRN** rolls d4: **2**

VAERA acts first.

*Morale check:* VAERA at +3 (under +15 threshold). BORRN at −3 (over −15 threshold). No die Power modifiers in effect for either.

---

### Round 2 — VAERA'S TURN (AP: 3/3)

VAERA has two Coins remaining, 10 Health, and 13 Stagger. BORRN is at 35 Health and 15 Stagger with no Power Guard remaining. This is VAERA's window.

**Action 1 (1 AP): Agile Melee attack.**

VAERA: **d8+4 → 6+4 = 10 Power.** Unopposed — BORRN has no Speed Die to respond with and no defensive die.

*Resolution:* BORRN takes **10 Health and 10 Stagger.**
- BORRN: 35 → **25 Health**. 15 → **5 Stagger.**

*Morale:* VAERA: +3 → **+6**. BORRN: −3 → **−6**.

**BORRN is now at 5 Stagger. One more Stagger hit and he goes down.**

---

**Action 2 (1 AP): Agile Melee attack.**

VAERA: **d8+4 → 7+4 = 11 Power.** Unopposed again.

*Resolution:* BORRN takes **11 Health and 11 Stagger.**
- BORRN's Stagger: 5 − 11 = below zero. **Stagger hits 0. BORRN is STAGGERED.**
- BORRN: 25 → **14 Health**.

**STAGGERED:** BORRN cannot use Skills. He loses all remaining Speed Dice. He will not regain AP at the start of his next turn. He cannot benefit from resistances. He cannot regain Stagger this Scene.

**VAERA regains 1 Coin** for Staggering an enemy. (2 → 3)

*Morale:* VAERA: +6 → **+9**. BORRN: −6 → **−9**.

---

**Action 3 (1 AP): Agile Melee attack.**

BORRN, Staggered, has no defense. VAERA closes in.

VAERA: **d8+4 → 5+4 = 9 Power.** Unopposed.

*Resolution:* BORRN takes 9 Health damage.
- BORRN: 14 → **5 Health.**

*Morale:* VAERA: +9 → **+12**. BORRN: −9 → **−12**.

---

### End State

| | VAERA | BORRN |
|---|---|---|
| Health | 10 | 5 |
| Stagger | 13 | 0 (Staggered) |
| Coins | 3 | 3 |
| Morale | +12 | −12 |
| Status | — | Staggered |

BORRN is Staggered, at 5 Health, with no defensive options. VAERA is low on Health but functional, Morale climbing toward the +15 threshold. The combat is over in all but formality.

---

### What This Example Demonstrated

- **Initiative:** Speed Die size determines order; higher AGI Grade means a bigger die and earlier turns.
- **AP management:** VAERA's 3 AP limited her to 2 attacks and 1 movement per turn. BORRN's 4 AP gave him one additional action.
- **Power Guard:** High-impact single-use defensive option. BORRN absorbed most of VAERA's first hit but has nothing left for the rest of the encounter.
- **Coin spend to retry:** A losing Clash can be reversed at the cost of a Coin. The Coin Bonus (+5 here) is substantial and swings outcomes meaningfully.
- **Unopposed attacks:** When a defender has no Speed Dice to expend, they eat the full die Power. Preserving Speed Dice is as important as spending them.
- **Evade (win):** A successful Evade recycles the die rather than dealing Stagger. VAERA avoided damage and kept her defensive option alive.
- **Evade (loss):** A failed Evade still reduces Stagger taken by its Power, softening the blow.
- **Morale tracking:** Every Clash result moved Morale. No threshold was crossed in this example, but BORRN came within 3 points of the −15 penalty and VAERA came within 3 of the +15 bonus. At higher levels or longer fights, Morale cascades become decisive.
- **Stagger accumulation → Staggered state:** BORRN's Stagger eroded steadily through multiple Unopposed hits. When it hit 0, he lost all function until Scene's end.
- **Coin gain from Staggering:** Combat rewards aggression — VAERA recovered a Coin by Staggering BORRN.

## Status Effect Template

The LLM generates Status Effects as needed during play. No fixed list exists — they emerge from the fiction, from specific weapon effects, environmental conditions, or unique enemy abilities. All Status Effects use this template.

```
**[Status Effect Name]**
Type: [DOT / Buff / Debuff / Special]
Trigger: [What applies it]
Effect: [What it does per stack or per activation]
Stacks: [Yes / No — if Yes, include max stack count]
Duration: [Scene / X Scenes / Until Removed]
Removal: [Condition to remove or deplete]
Morale: [None / +X per activation / −X per activation]
```

### Reference: Sinking

```
**Sinking**
Type: Debuff
Trigger: Applied through specific attack tags or skill effects
Effect: When hit, drain Morale equal to Potency, then reduce Count by 1
Stacks: Yes, Max 9 (Potency and Count tracked separately)
Duration: Until Count reaches 0
Removal: Count depletes naturally on each hit
Morale: −X per hit, where X equals current Potency
```

Sinking is the reference implementation. It demonstrates a Status Effect that works through Morale rather than Health or Stagger — a tool for breaking composure rather than the body. The distinction matters. A character can be walking and functional and still be losing the fight to Sinking.
